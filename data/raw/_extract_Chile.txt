# UHI Classification: Santiago, Chile


## Overview

The analysis focuses on accurately mapping the localized microclimates that define Urban Heat Islands through a workflow that extracts over 25 spectral indices from Sentinel-2, Landsat thermal, and DEM data at multiple resolutions, engineers physically motivated interaction features to capture urban heating dynamics, and addresses class overlap and imbalance using Quantile Transformation, with a Random Forest classifier applied to produce the final predictions.

---

## 1. Data Sources

We fuse three satellite sources, resampled to a common 100m grid:

* **Sentinel-2 (Optical, 13 bands):** Raw reflectance (B01–B12) at 10–60m native resolution. We apply strict cloud masking via the SCL band to exclude clouds, shadows, cirrus, nodata, and saturated pixels before generating a median composite for the time window.
* **Landsat 8/9 (Thermal + Optical):** Red, green, blue, nir08 (optical) + lwir11 (thermal infrared). Unlike the optical data, this uses a *single scene*. Thermal patterns fluctuate heavily day-to-day; compositing would blur the distinct UHI signal. `lwir11` provides the raw brightness temperature required for Land Surface Temperature (LST) derivation.
* **Copernicus DEM (Elevation):** 30m global elevation. This static feature is critical for capturing topographic cooling effects (lapse rates) in Santiago's varied terrain.

---

## 2. Derived Spectral Indices

From the raw Sentinel-2 bands, we compute 19 specialized spectral indices to isolate different land cover properties:

| Category | Indices | Purpose |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Vegetation** | NDVI, EVI, SAVI, GNDVI, LAI | Captures canopy cover and evapotranspirative cooling capacity. |
| **Water** | NDWI, MNDWI, NDMI | Identifies water bodies and soil moisture content. |
| **Built-up** | NDBI, ISA, BU, NDISI | Measures impervious surfaces and urbanization density. |
| **Soil/Bare** | NDBaI, BSI, DBSI | Differentiates bare ground and soil exposure from paved areas. |
| **Surface Energy** | LSE, Albedo, SWIR1_NIR, SWIR2_NIR | Captures the surface energy balance and reflectivity. |

**From Landsat Thermal:** We derive **LST (Land Surface Temperature)** via a mono-window method, estimating emissivity from the NDVI.  
*Note:* While critical, feature importance analysis confirms LST contributes ~8% to the model's decision-making—it is a significant, but not solely dominant, predictor when combined with structural indices.

---

## 3. Feature Engineering & EDA

To give the models a clearer mathematical separation of the classes, we engineered 6 interaction features. These are physically motivated formulas that highlight the contrasting drivers of urban heat:

| Feature | Formula | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **LST_x_NDVI** | LST × NDVI | Separates naturally hot bare land from cooler, vegetated areas. |
| **LST_x_NDBI** | LST × NDBI | Highlights temperatures amplified directly by dense built-up structures. |
| **elev_x_LST** | elevation × LST | Captures the topographic temperature lapse rate (higher elevations are inherently cooler). |
| **NDVI_minus_NDBI** | NDVI − NDBI | The contrast between vegetation and built-up areas (often the strongest single separator). |
| **LST_x_Albedo** | LST × Albedo | Captures the coupling between surface reflectivity and retained heat. |
| **BU_x_LST** | BU × LST | Built-up index weighted by local heat intensity. |

*Feature Pruning:* We explicitly dropped `NDVI_Landsat` due to its ~0.95 correlation with Sentinel's NDVI. Removing highly correlated, redundant features reduces noise and prevents over-weighting a single environmental signal.

### Exploratory Data Analysis: The "Medium" Class Challenge

During EDA, it became clear that the "High" (dense urban core) and "Low" (vegetated/elevated outskirts) classes separate relatively cleanly along standard indices. The primary modeling challenge is the **"Medium" class**—the transitional, suburban zones.

* **Topographic Influence:** In Santiago, elevation plays a massive role. High elevation areas consistently skew "Low" UHI, even if built-up.
* **Thermal Overlap:** The probability density of the "Medium" class heavily overlaps with both the lower end of the "High" class and the upper end of the "Low" class. It rarely dominates the probability space.

---

## 4. Modeling Strategy: Scaling & Algorithm Comparison

Because tree-based models split on individual feature thresholds, they handle correlated features well. However, to standardize the feature space and reduce the impact of outliers (like extreme isolated heat spikes), we apply a **Quantile Transformer** to map all features to a uniform distribution before training.

We evaluated two distinct approaches to handle the difficult classification space:

### Approach A: Gently Constrained Quantile Random Forest
* **The Theory:** A deeply grown Random Forest (`max_depth=20`) can learn highly specific, localized rules to separate the overlapping classes.
* **The Result:** While achieving high training accuracy, this approach struggles with generalization. Because the "Medium" class is transitional, a deep forest tends to overfit to specific pixel signatures rather than learning the broader physical rules of the transition zone.

### Approach B: Regularized Quantile XGBoost (The Chosen Model)
* **The Theory:** XGBoost builds trees sequentially to correct previous errors. By aggressively regularizing the model (`max_depth=4`, strong L1/L2 penalties), we force the model to learn simple, robust, physical thresholds rather than memorizing data points.
* **The Result:** The shallow, heavily regularized XGBoost model proved significantly more stable.
* **Cost-Matrix Threshold Optimization:** Because the model naturally struggles to confidently predict the "Medium" class, we implemented a custom threshold sweeper. By manually adjusting the probability threshold required to classify a pixel as "Medium," we penalize false "Medium" predictions. This optimization balances the precision/recall trade-off, forcing the model to make more definitive "High" or "Low" calls unless the physical evidence for a transitional "Medium" state is strong.

def results_summary(model, X_train, X_test, y_train, y_test, le, model_name="Model"):
    """Full train/test evaluation with gap analysis and classification report."""
    y_tr_enc = le.transform(y_train) if y_train.dtype == object else y_train
    y_te_enc = le.transform(y_test) if y_test.dtype == object else y_test

    pred_train = model.predict(X_train)
    pred_test = model.predict(X_test)

    f1_train = f1_score(y_tr_enc, pred_train, average="weighted")
    f1_test = f1_score(y_te_enc, pred_test, average="weighted")
    f1_gap = f1_train - f1_test

    acc_test = accuracy_score(y_te_enc, pred_test)
    prec_test = precision_score(y_te_enc, pred_test, average="weighted")
    rec_test = recall_score(y_te_enc, pred_test, average="weighted")

    auc = np.nan
    if hasattr(model, "predict_proba"):
        proba = model.predict_proba(X_test)
        if proba.shape[1] == len(le.classes_):
            auc = roc_auc_score(y_te_enc, proba, multi_class="ovr", average="weighted")

    print(f"{'='*60}")
    print(f"  {model_name} — Results Summary")
    print(f"{'='*60}")
    print(f"  F1 Train:     {f1_train:.4f}")
    print(f"  F1 Test:      {f1_test:.4f}")
    print(f"  F1 Gap:       {f1_gap:.4f}  {'⚠ HIGH' if abs(f1_gap) > 0.05 else '✓ stable'}")
    print(f"  Accuracy:     {acc_test:.4f}")
    print(f"  Precision:    {prec_test:.4f}")
    print(f"  Recall:       {rec_test:.4f}")
    print(f"  AUC (wt):     {auc:.4f}" if not np.isnan(auc) else "  AUC:          N/A")
    print(f"\nClassification Report:")
    print(classification_report(y_te_enc, pred_test, target_names=le.classes_))
    print(f"Confusion Matrix:")
    print(pd.DataFrame(
        confusion_matrix(y_te_enc, pred_test),
        index=[f"True_{c}" for c in le.classes_],
        columns=[f"Pred_{c}" for c in le.classes_]
    ))

    return {
        "model": model_name, "f1_train": f1_train, "f1_test": f1_test,
        "f1_gap": f1_gap, "accuracy": acc_test, "precision": prec_test,
        "recall": rec_test, "auc": auc,
    }

### Feature Engineering Functions

def engineer_features(df):
    df = df.copy()
    df["LST_x_NDVI"] = df["LST"] * df["NDVI"]
    df["LST_x_NDBI"] = df["LST"] * df["NDBI"]
    df["elev_x_LST"] = df["elevation"] * df["LST"]
    df["NDVI_minus_NDBI"] = df["NDVI"] - df["NDBI"]
    df["LST_x_Albedo"] = df["LST"] * df["Albedo"]
    df["BU_x_LST"] = df["BU"] * df["LST"]
    if "NDVI_Landsat" in df.columns:
        df.drop(columns=["NDVI_Landsat"], inplace=True)
    return df

def prepare_xy(df):
    df = engineer_features(df)
    drop_cols = [c for c in ["Latitude","Longitude","UHI_Class","label","resolution_m"] if c in df.columns]
    y = df["UHI_Class"] if "UHI_Class" in df.columns else None
    X = df.drop(columns=drop_cols)
    X = X.fillna(X.median()).replace([np.inf, -np.inf], np.nan).fillna(0)
    return X, y

## Initial Sweep & Model Selection
We evaluated 12 model architectures across 7 spatial resolutions (10m–1000m), including tree-based, distance-based (KNN), and probabilistic models. 

**Key Findings:**
* **KNN models** initially showed high test F1 scores at coarser resolutions (500–750m) but suffered from catastrophic overfitting (F1 gap = 0.16).
* **Tree-based models** (Random Forest, XGBoost) proved significantly more stable and generalized better.

**Selection Criteria:**
Models were ranked using a composite score that heavily penalized overfitting:
`Score = 0.7 × (Test F1) + 0.3 × (1 - |F1 Gap|)`
*Filter threshold:* |F1 Gap| < 0.05 and Test F1 > 0.60.

---

## The "Medium" Class Problem
The primary challenge in the Chile dataset is the "Medium" UHI class. Representing the transitional zone between hot urban cores and cool green areas, its spectral signal is inherently ambiguous. 

* **The Imbalance:** 50% of the Chile dataset is classified as "Medium," causing models to over-predict this class.
* **The Solution:** After testing 9 different approaches (including ordinal regression and ensembling), the winning combination was **Quantile Transformation paired with a Class-Weighted Random Forest**.

### Why QuantileTransformer Works
The `QuantileTransformer(output_distribution="uniform")` converts all feature values to their relative rank (0–1). 
* **Benefit:** It spreads out bunched data points in the middle of the distribution, giving the tree algorithms more mathematical space to define the ambiguous boundaries of the "Medium" class.
* **Limitation:** It destroys the absolute magnitude of physical metrics (like raw LST) and forces out-of-bounds transfer data (like the hotter tropical climate in Sierra Leone) to clip at 1.0.

---

## Feature Importance & Topographic Bias
Analysis of the Quantile Random Forest (@ 100m) revealed the top drivers for UHI classification in Chile:

| Feature | Importance | Interpretation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **elevation** | 0.168 | Topography is the strongest UHI predictor in Santiago. |
| **elev_x_LST** | 0.146 | Elevation-temperature interaction captures the lapse rate. |
| **LST** | 0.045 | Direct thermal signal. |
| **lwir11** | 0.043 | Raw thermal band (LST source). |

**Transferability Risk:** Elevation and its interaction term account for ~31% of the model's decision-making. Because Santiago sits in a mountain valley, the model relies heavily on topography. This poses a major generalization risk when transferring predictions to Sierra Leone (Freetown), which is a coastal, sea-level peninsula.

---

## Overfitting Diagnosis & Regularization Strategy
Initial testing revealed severe overfitting across the board. The unconstrained Random Forest essentially memorized the training data (Train F1: 0.986 vs. Test F1: 0.838 = **Gap: 0.148**).

**Root Cause:**
Trees were allowed to grow to unlimited depths, building hyper-specific, non-generalizable rules just to separate the massive "Medium" class from the noise.

**The Fix (Constraining Complexity):**
We aggressively regularized both models to force shallow, generalizable decision boundaries and drive the Train/Test F1 Gap below **0.05**.

* **Random Forest Constraints:**
  * `max_depth = 12` (Capped tree depth)
  * `min_samples_leaf = 25` (Prevents isolating tiny, zip-code specific clusters)
  * `max_samples = 0.8` & `max_features = "sqrt"` (Forces tree diversity via subsampling)

* **XGBoost Constraints:**
  * `max_depth = 4` (Forces highly generalized, shallow boundaries)
  * `learning_rate = 0.05` (Slower, conservative learning over 500 estimators)
  * `reg_alpha = 1.0`, `reg_lambda = 5.0` (Strong L1/L2 penalties on leaf weights)
  * `min_child_weight = 10` (Minimum samples required to create a new split)

# Feature Extraction (Assuming prepare_xy handles dropping labels, coords, etc.)
X, y = prepare_xy(train_test_100)

# Train/Test Split (70% Train, 30% Test)
X_tr, X_te, y_tr, y_te = train_test_split(
    X, y, test_size=0.2, stratify=y, random_state=42
)

# Encode target variables
y_tr_enc = le.transform(y_tr)
y_te_enc = le.transform(y_te)

# Feature Scaling (Quantile Transformer)
qt1 = QuantileTransformer(
    n_quantiles=min(1000, len(X_tr)),
    output_distribution="uniform", 
    random_state=42
)
X_tr_q = qt1.fit_transform(X_tr.values)
X_te_q = qt1.transform(X_te.values)

# Model Training: Heavily Constrained Quantile_RF @ 100m
print("Training RandomForestClassifier...")
m1 = RandomForestClassifier(
    n_estimators=500,            # Increased trees for smoother, more stable averaging
    class_weight="balanced",
    max_depth=20,                 # Kept shallow
    min_samples_split=25,        # NEW: Force a node to have at least 50 pixels before it can split
    max_features="sqrt",         # REDUCED: Forces trees to be diverse by only looking at a small random subset of features per split
    random_state=42,
    ccp_alpha=0.001,             # NEW REGULARIZATION: Cost-Complexity Pruning. Actively trims branches that don't add significant predictive power.
)
m1.fit(X_tr_q, y_tr_enc)

# --- NEW: Evaluation on BOTH Train and Test Splits ---
# Predictions
preds_tr_enc = m1.predict(X_tr_q)
preds_te_enc = m1.predict(X_te_q)

# Calculate F1 Scores
f1_train = f1_score(y_tr_enc, preds_tr_enc, average='weighted')
f1_test = f1_score(y_te_enc, preds_te_enc, average='weighted')
f1_gap = f1_train - f1_test

# Convert test predictions back to strings for the classification report
preds_te = le.inverse_transform(preds_te_enc)

print("\n--- Model Evaluation: Quantile_RF @ 100m (constrained) ---")

print(f"F1 Gap:         {f1_gap:.4f}  {'⚠ OVERFIT' if abs(f1_gap) > 0.05 else '✓ STABLE'}")

## Summary

**Key Findings:**

Based on the model evaluations and visual diagnostics, the pipeline reveals three critical takeaways:

1. Feature Engineering Drives Performance
The feature importance chart confirms that our custom, physically motivated interaction terms are the foundation of the model's success. The contrast between vegetation and urban density (NDVI_minus_NDBI) and the thermal-ecological interaction (LST_x_NDVI) overwhelmingly dominate the decision-making process, proving that engineered combinations are far more predictive than raw spectral bands alone.

2. The Inherent Ambiguity of the "Medium" Class
The confidence distribution boxplots visually diagnose the core classification bottleneck. While the model predicts the extreme "High" and "Low" classes with strong certainty (medians well above 0.6), the transitional "Medium" class lacks a distinct spectral signature. Its prediction probabilities cluster heavily around the 0.33 random-guess line, with massive overlap between true positives, false positives, and false negatives.

3. Regularization is Required over Complexity
Because the "Medium" transition zones are so spectrally ambiguous, giving a tree-based model unlimited depth simply allows it to memorize the noise of Santiago's specific layout (resulting in severe Train/Test F1 gaps). The visuals validate our final architectural pivot: the only way to build a model that can successfully transfer to a different topography like Sierra Leone is through aggressive structural regularization (shallow trees, strong subsampling) and cost-sensitive thresholding to manage the uncertain "Medium" boundaries.
